For both of us, actually. Two people who got there early, took it seriously, and never really stopped. Not as a hobby. As a way of thinking.

The through-line from a Spectrum to Claude Code isn't nostalgia. It's character. It's the instinct to pick up a new tool before the instructions exist, to understand it by using it, and to apply it to something that matters rather than something that performs.

You applied it to clinical language. In 1999. When nobody was calling it AI.

"The Comprehension Engine® isn't a response to the AI moment. It is the AI moment — just twenty-five years early."

Every startup that raised a Series A in 2023 to build ambient voice technology for the NHS is writing thought leadership posts about how AI is going to transform clinical documentation. You were building the thing they're writing about before most of their founders were in secondary school.

That's not a heritage story. That's the actual story.

III — The gap

Every AVT system in the NHS owns the same problem.

They capture what was said. They structure it. They validate it against the transcript. They produce a clinical note. And then the consultation ends.

And the patient leaves.

With a diagnosis, perhaps. A prescription, sometimes. A referral, maybe. But no clear path through what comes next — no sequenced, human-centred route from the end of the clinical encounter to the next meaningful point of support.

The navigation gap

The problem isn't information. NHS resources exist. Local services exist. Support structures exist. The problem is that nobody has designed the moment of handoff — the space between "the consultation is over" and "the patient knows what to do next."

That space is currently filled by a print-out, a website link, or the patient's own ability to navigate a system that was never designed to be navigated.

Support Playlists closes that gap. Not with a chatbot. Not with a portal. With a sequenced, intelligible, human-centred set of next steps — generated from what was said, calibrated to where the person is, delivered at the moment it's needed.

Every AVT vendor in the market is competing on the same axis: transcription accuracy, SOAP note formatting, MHRA compliance, integration depth. Important work. Solved work, increasingly.

Nobody is competing on what happens after the note is written. That space is open. And it belongs, naturally, to the company that has owned the clinical encounter for twenty-five years.

IV — The elegance

Platform agnostic.
Partner specific.

Support Playlists doesn't require Lexacom infrastructure. It could run on any AVT platform. That's important to say clearly, because it defines exactly what the opportunity is.

What this means for IP

The framework stays with Neighbourhood CoLab. The logic, the sequencing, the Emotional Architecture layer. Lexacom licences the capability — it doesn't acquire it. Clean. Durable. Protects both parties through any future transition.

What this means for market position

Being first isn't about exclusivity. It's about the evidence base, the case studies, the clinical credibility of having done it first — in a real PCN, with real patients, in a real NHS context. That's worth more than a patent.

The VC-backed competitors are racing each other on transcription accuracy. They are not thinking about what happens after the note is written. They will — eventually. But by then, Lexacom will have been doing it for long enough that the question of who invented this space has already been answered.

"First to market with Support Playlists isn't a feature launch. It's a legacy statement."
